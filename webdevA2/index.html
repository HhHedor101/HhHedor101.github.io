History of Military Tanks (and other related Vehicles!)

Contents

War is something that humanity has waged for all of its existence. It is during war that people would want to find the most efficient way of prematurely ending their enemies' lives, resulting in some interesting innovations. One of these innovations is the concept of "Armoured Fighting Vehicles", which came about in the middle of the period of industrial warfare.

"Industrial warfare"

The term industrial warfare refers to a period of time that began around the same the time the Industrial Revolution began, and ends around the beginning of the Atomic Age (marked by the detonation of the first prototype nuclear bomb during the Trinity test).

It got its name from the fact that countries began having the ability to equip their armed forces, by using the extreme efficiency of factories to rapidly produce munitions. Tanks are one example of this, as they too were, and still are, produced in very large quantities.

As technology progresses and militaries find new ways of waging war, armoured fighting vehicles have also evolved greatly with their continued use in conflicts today - let's look into how this evolution has taken place!

Early Conceptions

The idea to have some sort of fortified moving vehicle be used in war is a concept that has actually existed for hundred of years - All the way back in the 15th Century, Leonardo Da Vinci (yes, the painter) was credited with designing something that is very identical in function to a tank.

People only really started taking the idea seriously when the Industrial Revolution began, which was when internal combustion engines became a big thing - the notion of a big metal vehicle that was armed was suddenly plausible. Ideas were especially abundant in the lead-up to the First World War, with design proposals floating all over Europe.

The first physical functioning design was the Lipt치k harckocsi, or simply the Liptak tank - a Hungarian tracked vehicle by a guy called P치l Lipt치k. His design unfortunately was rejected by the Hungarian military as they deemed it too unprotected to be used practically.

World War I (1914 - 1918)

Even with its centuries-long origins, tanks really only began to manifest during the Great War. The First World War was a conflict that was especially grim and hard to swallow. You might have seen some photographs or other depictions of the battlefields - gruesome fields of dark brown mud with puddles that were sometimes more blood than water, the barbed wire and abandoned bodies of deceased soldiers and horses who could not be evacuated strewn around everywhere, and the general ruins of former housing as well.

The nature of trench warfare during WWI meant that soldiers on both sides were forced to charge headlong into almost certain death by gunfire. This was the unfortunate fate of many men during the war. Because of this, the idea of trying to develop a vehicle that could withstand conventional gunfire whilst also providing offensive capabilities once again became very popular. Many countries involved in the war (France, Great Britain, the German Empire, the Russian Empire etc.) tried pioneering their own designs of the first warships-on-land - the 'Landship'.

Did You Know?

The term 'tank' came from a code word! The idea of landships was kept as a secret on the British side, so developers decided to call their prototype 'water carriers' - disguising the armoured vehicles as simply vehicles to transport water, so that their developments remained a secret from the enemy. The code word of 'water tank' was used throughout development, and eventually it stuck as a way to continue deceiving the opposing Germans as to their true purpose.

After a long period of development, the British finally introduced their first working tanks to the battlefield - In September 1916, a few new 'Mark I' tanks saw their first action at the Battle of Flers-Courcelette which was part of the Somme Offensive (one of the biggest and deadliest battles in WWI). Although many broke down in the middle of their attack, beginning able to withstand heavy gunfire and cut through barbed wire meant that a whole third of the attacking force broke through no man's land. Thus began the era of armoured warfare - its birthplace in trenches of Northern France.

The Somme had only seen a small number of Mark Is participate - the first time tanks were used en masse was at the Battle of Cambrai in 1917, which saw more than 400 Mark IVs (an evolution of the earlier Mark I) go over the top. However, mechanical difficulties and German resistance caused half of all these tanks to be knocked out of action in one day.


Although the Mark Is did not perform very well in the Somme Offensive, public reactions exploded - British media compared the fearsome weapons to dragons, whales and mammoths. Many articles and documentaries were made on tanks, which saw widespread viewership. Eventually other countries developed their own tank designs - French and German designs would see combat in the latter years of the war, with the German A7V eventually butting heads with the Mark IVs at the Second Battle of Villers-Bretonneux, the first ever tank-on-tank battle. At some points, successful German attacks even allowed them to capture some British tanks and turn them on their former masters.

Other Countries' Tank Designs

Being a totally new concept meant countries got more creative with their designs. As we have seen, the Mark Is had the layout of having the driver (the driving compartment) be placed at the front, with guns (the fighting compartment) placed at the sides of tanks, and finally the engine compartment at the back. Other countries tried to vary this design - such as putting the fighting compartment at the front of the tank instead, as seen with the German A7V or the French 'Saint-Chamond'. Some came up with very... interesting designs, like the Russian Tsar Tank.

However, it could be said that the most impactful tank design was the French Renault FT light tank, which was the first tank to follow the driving-fighting-engine compartment layout with the turret on top instead - This is the layout tanks follow to this day.

The Interwar Period (1919 - 1938)

In the years between both world wars, tank development still took place despite the time of relative peace. Countries' designs became more distiguishable from each other as they developed different doctrines for armoured warfare.


France and Great Britain continued on with their research, and both came up with different ideas of how they were going to best make use of their newly-pioneered inventions.


Great Britain started by improving on their Great War designs, making their tanks move faster and giving them greater traversal with tanks like the Vickers Medium Mark II. British experiments eventually came up with three main types of main tanks - 'Light', 'Cruiser' and 'Infantry', each fulfilling their own roles such as providing fast-moving destructive capability or acting as shields for regular infantry.

France had a somewhat similar system, although calling their two tank types 'Infantry' and 'Cavalry' tanks instead. They developed many unique designs and was actually the second largest producer of tanks - behind the Soviet Union. By the 1930s, France had constructed possibly the best fleet of tanks at the time to arm themselves, from the two-manned, small-sized Renault R 35 to behemoths like the Char B1.

However, French tanks often could not perform very well due to their crew size. France suffered a great loss in population in the First World War, which left their military short-staffed. As a result, tanks often had very small crew sizes (Light tanks often carried only two crewmen) which forced tankmen to carry a very heavy set of tasks, which no doubt tired them out and reduced their effectiveness.


The Soviet Union also followed similar 'Light'/'Heavy' conventions - small and fast tanks with larger and more sturdy ones. Soviet heavy tanks during this period notably had multiple turrets on a single chassis, like the T28 or the T35. This was a design philosophy more reminiscent of WWI, since tanks at the time were mainly used to spearhead frontal assaults and involved attacking multiple enemy positions at once.

At this time, the Treaty of Versailles limited the military of the German Weimar Republic, including research into armoured warfare. It was only when Adolf Hitler came into power as Fuhrer in 1934 that tank development continued, which gave birth to the tanks that would later on be used in the invasions of Poland and France in the early stages of World War II.

World War II (1939 - 1945)

When talking about tanks, it is almost impossible not to talk about tanks during the Second World War. This was the war that armoured warfare had truly began solidifying itself as an important weapon in warfare in general. Tanks became omnipresent, with almost every military that participated fielding their own vehicles in some way. They also started using them in conjunction with other elements. Many tanks were developed and fielded over the course of the 6 years - Let's cover the more significant ones.

The Early German Panzers

From the very beginning of the war in September 1939, the 'panzers' (German term for tanks) of the Wehrmacht had been crossing the German border into Poland. These included the Panzer Is, Panzer IIs, Panzer IIIs and Panzer IVs. The Panzer IIIs and IVs are of particular importance, as they were intended as Nazi Germany's two main tanks, each fulfilling anti-tank and infantry support roles respectively. However, these two types were available in scarce numbers, and Germany's tank fleet comprised of mostly the earlier and weaker Panzer Is and Panzer IIs instead.

The Panzer IVs are particularly noteworthy. They are probably one of Germany's all-around best designs, having been used from the start in 1939 all the way till the end of the war in Europe in May 1945. The design went through many phases in its evolution, with each phase being marked by a letter of the alphabet - from the earlier Ausf칲hrung A all the way till the Ausf칲hrung J. They were also used in every single theatre Germany was involved in - in France and the rest of Western Europe, the Soviet Union and North Africa.

Queen of the Desert

Tanks were also a major weapon used in the North Africa campaign, which ran from 1940 - 1943. The campaign initially saw British forces from Egypt butting heads with Italian forces from the Libyan colony. Of particular note is a British infantry tank called the Matilda II, with its most impressive aspect being its armour - in the earlier years, its thick armour plating meant it was almost (but not completely) invulnerable to Axis firepower! Matilda tanks of the British 7th Armoured Division laid waste to Italian forces in Egypt. Matilda tanks also was part of the force that saw Italian forces pushed all the way back to Libya - it even gained a nickname: 'Queen of the Desert'. These so-called royalty continued to be a pain all the way till late 1941, even after the Germans arrived to strengthen the opposition.

However, the Matilda's reign would not last long - as speed, essential in the rapid manoeuvre warfare practised in the deserts, was something she painfully lacked. Eventually, the German Afrika Korps brought over guns like their 8.8cm flak anti-aircraft gun, or the 5cm Pak 36 and 7.5cm Pak 40, which could knock out Matildas. Her effectiveness fizzled out, and she eventually reached the end of her service life around late 1942 when designs like the 'Valentine' or 'Churchill' tanks took her place instead.

The T-34

The Soviet T-34 might be one of the most legendary tanks of all-time, it was superior in many aspects - it's 76.2mm gun was superior to many other tank guns at the time and its sloped armour provided solid protection. It was produced in great numbers, being the most produced tank of WWII and influencing tank design for many years. It has one of the longest service lives for a tank, with some tanks still being in service with countries' militaries today.

On top of it being one best all-around tanks for its time, it was also produced in extremely large numbers - with more than 80,000 T-34 tanks of all variants being built. Because of the urgency of having to defend themselves, Soviet factories would often skip producing certain parts deemed unnecessary, like headlights. There were times when factories were so close to the frontlines that crews could literally drive fresh T-34s from the factory to the battlefields without transportation, often without their regular paintjobs. During the German invasion of the Soviet Union, when encountering Soviet T-34 squads, German armoured units would often find themselves outnumbered by T-34s.


It's advantages in firepower, protection and numbers made it a force to be truly reckoned with at the Eastern Front.

The M4 Sherman

The Allies' other Jack-of-all-Trades tank is the M4 Sherman, named after American Civil War general William Tecumseh Sherman - think of it as the American alternative to the T-34. In terms of individual performance, The Sherman was actually weaker than the T-34, sporting a smaller gun and lesser armour. However, like the T-34, it was produced in very big quantities, with almost 50,000 being produced. It went through many iterations, and saw combat in almost every theatre in WWII - France, the Soviet Union, North Africa and even against the Imperial Japanese.

The Sherman might not have been very great on its own, but it had many unique strengths - particularly in logistics. The tank was very well liked among crews and frontline troops. The tank was large and spacious unlike the T-34 which was extremely cramped, and it had many entrance/exit hatches. All this meant crews were able to operate it quite comfortably, and could also make a quick escape if the tank was knocked out or on fire. When an Allied Sherman DID get knocked out, crews could return to their lines and almost immediately find a replacement waiting for them. The same could be actually be said for almost all pieces of Western Allied equipment - such was the almost phenomenal logistics system the Allies had in place.

Late-war German Panzers

German tanks later on in the war are particularly notorious - most designs past 1943 had many influences (but are not direct products) from Hitler's idealistic aspirations for a 'Wunderwaffe' - he demanded his armoured force have a fleet of tanks that exaggerated firepower and protection. The most famous tank from this period of time is the Panzer V - otherwise known as the Tiger I. It had a reputation for having a gun that could knock out most Allied tanks, armour that protected against most Allied firepower and could still keep up with its contemporaries in mobility and speed. Other tanks, such as the Panzer VI - Panther and the Panzer VII - Tiger II or 'King Tiger' that had similarly superior design philosophies in firepower and protection. Although their reputation is somewhat overblown, which, to be fair, is a notion that has been debunked in recent years, the Allies were still right to fear the formidable tanks.

However, almost all of them greatly suffered from similar issues stemming from Nazi Germany's situation late in the war. Germany's logistics system, exhausted from years of conflict, had largely depleted its resources. For example, Germany had fewer oil fields and sources of steel and synthetic fuel to draw materiel from. Additionally, the urgency of Germany's situation also meant things like crew training had to be extremely rushed. As such, German tanks could not perform very effectively and often ran out of fuel or ammunition - in a lot of cases, crews had to completely abandon their vehicles when their tank eventually broke down - a polar opposite of the Allied side's situation. Their extremely big size and weight also limited their reach, especially in remote areas like the Belgian or French countryside, or when bridge crossing was necessary. Therefore, while fearsome in theory, tanks like the Tigers or Panthers often had very poor practical effectiveness.

Other Axis Tanks

We have talked a lot about tanks from Germany, but the Axis powers also includes Imperial Japan and Fascist Italy - what about their tanks? When it comes to these two other powers, their tanks were, to be blunt, not good. Both nations did own their own fleet of armoured vehicles, but they had almost no chance of matching up against the main Allied powers.


Starting with the Japanese, their tanks were not good because they did not put much effort into armoured warfare research. The most produced Japanese tank during WWII was a light tank called the Type 95 'Ha-Go'. This tank was not powerful - its gun was only 37mm thick and its thickest armour is 12mm thick, which is extremely thin compared to the American armour it would realistically face in the Pacific.
However, there is a reason the Japanese did not bother developing their tanks - they did not need to. When it came to fighting the Americans, the Japanese relied on the strengths of their regular infantry, naval air arms and especially their navy, all of which were extremely potent in combatting the Americans. Tanks were not a necessity for the kind of war the Japanese were fighting against the Americans. Moreover, Japan's other enemies included China and the colonial forces of the French and the British - none of which had equipment that could outmatch the force of the invading Japanese. Therefore, it can be said that Japan did not have powerful tanks simply because ppwerful tanks were not necessary - tanks like the Type 95 were already sufficient.

While Japan's situation was understandable, the same could not be said for Fascist Italy. The M13/40 tank was intended to be the main tank used by the Royal Italian Army - it was actually slightly more powerful than the previously mentioned Japanese Type 95, with its thickest armour being 42mm thick and its main gun being 47mm in diameter. While not bad on paper, the Italian armoured fleet actually mostly consisted of 'tankettes' like the L3/33, which, honestly, could barely be considered a tank. Furthermore, Italian tanks were seldom fit with radios, their crews received almost no training, and unit formations had zero cohesion with each other.

This is actually a recurring theme across the Royal Italian Army during WWII. Italian industry was very small compared to its enemies, and it had used a lot of its resources in preceding wars, like during its conquest of Ethiopia in 1935 and its intervention in the 1936 Spanish Civil War. The Under-Secretary for War Production claimed that Italy could not possibly be ready for a war until at least October 1942, yet the military was rushed to war in 1940, under the command of generals who were expecting the tactics of WWI-style trench warfare. The Royal Italian Army was, admittedly, a case of incompetent planning and almost non-existent logistics.

The Cold War & Afterwards

After the end of WWII, tank development slowed down drastically since there was no longer the threat of world domination by a single dictatorship. But it did not stop completely, as ideologies clashed between East and West, and the Cold War began. The Western powers and the Soviet Union continued improving their tanks as they fought the many proxy wars characteristic of the Cold War, making them faster, harder-hitting, and more armoured.


A particularly notable early-Cold War tank is the Soviet T-54/55 series of tanks, conceived at the end of WWII and first put into service in 1947. It is actually the most-produced tank in history, with estimations indicating almost a whopping 100,000 being produced. This design of tank would and still be the basis for the tank fleets Russia fields to this day. Many smaller countries still use the 70-year-old tank, although usually with some modernizations to update the old design. The tank was exported all across the world, with countries in South America, the Middle East and Asia still having some in service.

Another development during the 1950s was the widespread adoption of the 'main battle tank' concept. 'Main battle tanks' were usually derived from the medium tanks of the time, which sought to strike a balance between speed, firepower and protection. Tanks eventually reached a point where their guns could penetrate any practical level of armour. This meant that the class of tanks called 'heavy tanks' - intended to carry the biggest guns and thickest armour, was rendered obsolete. 'Light tanks' which had little firepower and armour also fell out of style as it was realized that no amount of speed could compensate for both the offset qualities. Thus militaries began standardizing their tank fleets with fewer separate tank designs in service at any given moment.

The previously mentioned T-54/55 tank was one of the Soviet Union's first main battle tanks. Another example of a wildly successful 'MBT' was the British Centurion, introduced in 1945 after WWII. It too has an extremely long and ongoing service life and is a popular choice of tank for export, although it was not mass-produced on the scale the T-54/55 was, having less thank 5,000 units built.


Skipping ahead to around the late 1970s, this was when the prototypes for the tanks in service today would be coming out. In November 1976, the US Department of Defense would award the Chrysler Defense company a $4.9B USD contract to begin producing the finalized design of the XM1, which is now known as the M1 Abrams main battle tank - this is the tank that would be an icon of late and post-Cold War American interventionism, especially in the Middle East. The tank would appear a lot in Iraq and Afghanistan.

At around the same time in 1979, the first Leopard 2s were being pressed into service by the West German military. This tank is still in service in Germany today, being on its eight iteration - the 'Leopard 2A8'. The Leopard 2 also happens to be a very popular export design, with many other NATO nations procuring some of their own and modifying it to their needs. Sweden built some of their own Leopards, calling it the 'Stridsvagn 122'. Poland bought some of the older 'Leopard 2A4' tanks from Germany and mdoernized them, dubbing it the 'Leopard 2PL'.

Singapore's Armoured Fleet

Singapore also possesses a formidable tank force - one of the most powerful in South-East Asia! The main battle tank used is the Leopard 2SG, which you can probably tell is an upgraded Leopard 2 tank from Germany. Some domestic designs are in service as well, such as the Bionix infantry fighting vehicle which has been in service with the Singapore Armed Forces since 1997. There is also the Hunter Armoured Fighting Vehicle, a new armoured fighting vehicle that is apparently the 'world's first fully digitalised platform'.

The Classification of Tanks

Throughout this whole article, you would have heard tanks being referred to as 'light tanks', 'medium tanks' or 'heavy tanks' particularly in the WWII section, whilst the Cold War onwards used terms like 'main battle tank' or 'infantry fighting vehicle'. This section aims to explain all these terms!

Light, Medium and Heavy Tanks

These are the terms that were mainly used to classify tanks before, during and a little bit after WWII. Alongside describing their weight and size, these types also worked to briefly describe the combat role the tank of that type would most likely be assigned;

1. Light tanks were, as the name implies, smaller and lighter tanks that usually carried less armour and firepower, in exchange for being highly mobile. They were usually assigned less confrontational roles, like armoured reconnaissance or artillery observation. They were much more prevalent in the 1930s and 1940s, but some light tank designs did exist in the Cold War, like the American 'High-Survivability Test Vehicle - Light'.

2. Medium tanks are the middle-ground of performance aspects of tanks, balancing mobility, firepower and protection. They are usually employed as the bread-and-butter of armoured fleets, engaging in more direct combat against infantry - more well-known tanks usually fall under this category. Examples include some tanks previously mentioned in this article, like the M4 Sherman, T-34 or Panzer IV. Main battle tanks would soon succeed the medium tank in their roles.

3. Heavy tanks are the polar opposite of light tanks, trading mobility for greater protection and firepower. Their role was to attack and destroy heavily fortified enemy positions to create openings for the infantry; They were also sometimes used as mobile bunkers. The German Tiger Is and Tiger IIs are considered heavy tanks - other examples include the Soviet KV and IS series of tanks.

Tank Destroyers and Other Self-Propelled Weapons

Tanks are typically designed to engage both enemy infantry and other tanks. However, there are other tanks that have more specialized qualities. Tank destroyers, as the name implies, is a class of vehicles intended specifically to destroy even the heaviest types of tanks. The nature of the role means their designs totally commit to firepower - they can be somewhat vulnerable to other types of attack, like against infantry or air attack. They were an almost uniquely WWII feature and typically did not typically have turrets. Tank destroyers also existed in some way during the Cold War, as vehicles that carried guided missiles instead of a conventional gun.


There are also self-propelled artillery vehicles. While they look a lot like tanks, and are considered as tanks, they do not really perform like tanks. Self-propelled artillery is simply when designers put an artillery cannon on a tank-like chassis. They also operate much like artillery - positioning themselves far away from the frontlines and raining suffering down on any unfortunate victims. Having an artillery gun have its own tank body helps it reposition to a more advantageous location much quicker, and there are still self-propelled artillery vehicles being used today. Along with normal guns, this can also include rocket and missile launchers.


On the other hand, there is the self-propelled anti-aircraft class of vehicles. Like the artillery variant, these are vehicles that carry anti-aircraft armament instead, and also include both guns and missiles. Having the ability to stay on the move means you are less of a sitting duck asking to get bombed.

Infantry and Cavalry Fighting Vehicles

The infantry fighting vehicle is a concept that began to grow in popularity during the Cold War and is a type still used in modern militaries. It has a role similar to Armoured Personnel Carriers - to carry troops into battle. The main difference is that IFVs actually join the troops it carries into combat, unlike APCs which have guns solely for self-defense. IFVs are also capable of engaging any ground target on its own due to the variety of weaponry the type typically carries.


"(An IFV is) an armoured fighting vehicle which is designed and equipped primarily to transport a combat infantry squad, and which is armed with an integral or organic cannon of at least 20 millimeters calibre and sometimes an antitank missile launcher" - The 1990 Treat on Conventional Armed Forces in Europe, on the definition of an IFV.


The term 'Cavalry Fighting Vehicle' is a term that comes from the American M3 Bradley Cavalry Fighting Vehicle. It is very similar to its older sister, the M2 IFV, also called the Bradley. It is different in that it has fewer acommodations for infantry passengers, but carries more ammunition and has a different role from the M2 (armoured reconnaissance rather than infantry support).

More Interesting Tanks and Tank Technology

Here are some more very interesting tank-related technology and notable tanks throughout history!

Antonov A-40

The A-40 was a WWII Soviet experimental FLYING tank!
Not really - the idea was that a bomber carrying a Soviet T-60 light tank with biplane wings would drop the tank on the battlefield, so that the tank could glide to a position close to its objective. This idea was never practical though - there was a very fundamental problem in testing. In order for any plane at the time to be able to carry a tank, Soviet testers were forced to strip the tank of all of its ammunition, and could only use a tiny amount of fuel. Thus if this concept was applied, even if the glider tank did survive the fall with its crew, there was no way it could inflict any significant damage or complete any objective before running out of resources. In fact, it would most likely only cause issues, since there would then be a tank crew stuck behind enemy lines and in-need of rescuing.
Technology would reach a point when planes could carry even the heaviest tanks with more reliability. But hot-dropping them in combat would most likely remain impractical and unnecessary.

Deep Fording

In WWII, Allied tanks could have 'duplex drives' that allow them to float above water. Today, tanks can actually move around underwater, simply by attaching a snorkel to the tank which provides a path of air circulation. This technique is particularly invaluable in Europe, as NATO and Soviet forces would encounter many rivers where bridges may be destroyed or otherwise inadequate for use by heavy vehicles like tanks. Crews tend to be a little nervous about this method of water-crossing, as they are physically trapped inside the tank. If a snorkelled tank broke down in the middle, the crews would have to stay inside until it is towed above water.

Active Protection Systems

As weapons become more advanced, so did the protection systems on tanks. Tanks no longer need to rely solely on thicker skin to shield them from harm, as there have been recent developments of gadgets that can neutralize or completely destroy incoming anti-tank missiles. These 'active protection systems' have two categories - 'soft-kill' and 'hard-kill'.

'Soft-kill' is a system in place to confuse or otherwise mess with an incoming missile's tracking - missiles are not effective if they cannot hit their target accurately. Gadgets such as smoke screens or 'dazzlers', which disrupt missile guidance with radiation, are considered soft-kill countermeasures.
'Hard-kill' countermeasures, on the other hand, seek a more complicated but proven method - physically pulverizing an incoming missile until it is dust. A simple way to think of it is that there is an automated turret on a tank that, when it sees a missile coming, tracks the missile and shoots a shotgun shell at the missile. This can prematurely detonate the missile before it comes into contact with the protected tank. It is like a miniature missile defense system for the tank.
